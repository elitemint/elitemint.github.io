As humans, we tend to compare our abilities to others and this ends up making us feel better or worse about ourselves.

The big-fish‚Äìlittle-pond effect refers to how individuals in a less capable group have higher self-concepts than those equally capable individuals in a highly capable group when comparing themselves with their peers.
In the educational context, the research has found that high-achieving students (big fish) in lower-achieving schools (little pond) have a more positive academic self-concept.
Compared to those equally talented students (big fish) who are in high-achieving environments (big pond).
This shows that even high achievers and gifted students are just as susceptible to self-doubt depending on the achievements of their reference group.
That is, they tend to view their abilities better when in a group of relatively low achievers than when in a group of high achievers.
The effect has been well-known among education professionals as the phenomenon consistently appeared in all subjects at all levels of education, affecting both girls and boys equally. Countries all around the world see it with people in all income levels exhibiting such effect.
